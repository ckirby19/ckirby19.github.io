LIGO Laser Light Generation

  • Company/Group: Laser Interferometer Gravitational-Wave Observatory, MIT Kavli Group
  • My Role: Research Assistant
  • Dates: May 2017 - August 2017

LIGO is the largest gravitational wave observatory in the world, made famous after the first observation of gravitational waves in 2016. The Kavli Institute at MIT conducts ways to improve the gravitational wave detector so that we can understand more and more about our universe

Green and Red laser frequencies are needed in the experiment, but current power efficiency in the conversion between frequencies is not optimised. Better efficiency would allow parts of the LIGO experiment to run at lower power levels.

  • Apply standard methods to suppress RF noise and interference
  • Use a Pound-Drever-Hall control loop
  • Control for temperature changes and vibrations on the optics table
  • Find the peak resonance point for the laser to generate green light efficiently

To breifly explain the thought process for the experiment, a red laser source is manipulated using mirrors and lenses to focus it into a "bow tie cavity", a set of 4 mirrors where laser light can enter. The distance between these 4 mirrors can be modified very precisely, and at certain lengths the red light will experience resonance and a phenomena called Second Harmonic Generation to produce green light too. Both of these frequencies then leave the cavity, to be used in the rest of the experiment. To lock the mirrors so that these resonant distances can be maintained constantly, one of the mirrors is attached to a piezo crystal that moves the mirror based on an error signal, creating a feedback loop.
