MIT 2.007 Moonshot

  • Class Details: 2.007: Design and Manufacturing I
  • Dates: February 2019 - May 2019

A project-based class for undergraduate students that culminates in an end-of-semester robotics competition. It places robots – each built by a singular student using a universal kit of tools and components – head to head during an event. A stage is created for each student to navigate their robot around, with an RC controller or autonomously for more points, and complete different tasks in order to obtain points. Different ways to collect points included collecting rocks from the ground, rotating a handle as fast as possible, and pulling a latch.

My focus was on how to collect rocks quickly and efficiently. I used a Pugh table to decide what overall game strategy to use, and then to decide what method to use to collect the rocks.

The process for both the robot as a whole and the rock collecting component can be seen visually below. The initial work on the robot was focused on understanding the basics of sheet metal forming and robot construction. I used a simple two-wheel drive system with a caster wheel at the front, which was soon replaced with a turned piece of Delrin to reduce friction and improve mobility. Since the competition required bringing the collected rocks up hill, back to the starting point, I soon changed to a four wheel drive system to cope with the increased weight of the robot. I then redesigned the robot to move the centre of mass further back, to make it easier for the robot to climb back up the ramp.

The rock collecting component was inspired by numerous roller inlet designs from other robot competitions. Rubber bands provided an easy to build and adaptive method for collecting rocks. The initial concept was created using laser cut acrylic pieces, connected with a steel hexagonal rod and separated using hollow aluminium tubing. The rubber bands were initially placed straight on the device, but with some early testing I found that twisting the rubber bands, to create a parabolic shape, would better conform to different rock shapes. I created a simple rig to test the concept, and then added a motor to test the device running continuously.

In practice, my robot was able to collect 7-8 rocks in time and return to the starting point. In the competition, however, unfortunately the rocks were placed very close together, which we did not know would happen, and so my robot was not very well prepared for that. I managed to collect 2 rocks, but as can be seen in the video below, in dramatic fashion, I didn't quite make it! Oh well, the most important thing was the learning process and the experience of my first robotics competition!
